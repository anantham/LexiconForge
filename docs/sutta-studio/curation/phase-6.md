## Concerns flagged

### Translator citations
The four nibbāna renderings (Bodhi untranslated / Sujato extinguishment /
Thanissaro unbinding / common Nirvana) are widely documented and I'm ~95%
confident on each. The sacchikiriyāya renderings (especially Thanissaro's
"direct experiencing") are ~80% confident — I'd want to verify the exact
phrasing in his MN10 translation before publishing.

### nibbāna is high-stakes
This is THE goal-term of the entire canon. The four-tradition rendering
surfaces real disagreement. If we ever build a "nibbāna" landing page or
glossary entry, this is the foundational definition. The post-phantom-purge
format gives the reader the four traditions without picking a winner.

## What this phase teaches about the formula

Phase 6 is where the formula CLOSES. The five datives-of-purpose form
a complete arc:

| Phase | Word | Sense |
|---|---|---|
| 2 | sattānaṁ visuddhiyā | For the **purification** of beings |
| 3 | sokaparidevānaṁ samatikkamāya | For the **surmounting** of grief & lament |
| 4 | dukkhadomanassānaṁ atthaṅgamāya | For the **disappearance** of suffering & dejection |
| 5 | ñāyassa adhigamāya | For the **attainment** of the method/path |
| 6 | nibbānassa sacchikiriyāya | For the **realization** of nibbāna |

Five movements: purify → surmount → vanish → attain → realize. The verbs
escalate from negative (purify, surmount, vanish) to positive (attain,
realize). The grammatical numbers track: plural (worldly multiplicity,
phases 2-4) → singular (path-and-goal unity, phases 5-6).

This is the formula's architecture made audible. The Buddha names the
purpose of the practice in a single tightly-constructed sentence; the
grammar IS the doctrine.
