## Polyglot Lexicography and Concept Grounding of the Prajñāpāramitā Hṛdaya Sūtra

The Prajñāpāramitā Hṛdaya Sūtra (Heart Sutra) remains a foundational text of Mahāyāna Buddhism, distinguished by its radical deconstruction of Abhidharma categories through the lens of emptiness (śūnyatā). The following lexicographical analysis establishes an authoritative, cross-language concept-grounding framework for thirty core tenets within the sutra, mapped across major canonical languages to assist in the development of a polyglot reader.

### 1. prajñā (wisdom)

- **84000**: https://84000.co/glossary/41924
- **DDB**: 般若 — The Digital Dictionary of Buddhism defines this as discriminating knowledge, intuitive wisdom, or the highest form of cognition in Mahāyāna Buddhism. It transcends dualistic subject-object conceptualization to directly apprehend the emptiness of all phenomena, serving as the sixth and culminating perfection that guides the other five.
- **Princeton**: s.v. "prajñā", p. 655

**Contested:** no
**Notes:** In Mahāyāna doctrine, prajñā is strictly distinguished from jñāna (conventional knowledge or resulting pristine awareness). While jñāna often refers to the resultant state of a Buddha's omniscient awareness, prajñā represents the active, non-dual discriminative awareness required to realize emptiness along the path, ultimately perfected as prajñāpāramitā. The text's entire epistemic framework is anchored on this direct, unmediated cognition. Chinese translators chose phonetic transliteration (*pan-njak in Middle Chinese Baxter-Sagart reconstruction) because existing Chinese terms for wisdom (like 智 zhì) carried heavy Confucian and Daoist baggage that implied conventional, worldly intellect. By transliterating the term, they preserved its sacred, unprecedented metaphysical weight. The Tibetan translators, conversely, employed a highly precise calque: shes (knowing) + rab (supreme), effectively neutralizing the risk of semantic drift while avoiding a meaningless phonetic loan. The Heart Sutra is named for this specific concept, representing the "heart" or "core" of this supreme knowing.

**Contested:** yes (etymological derivation)
**Notes:** The term contains a famously contested dual etymology. Modern Western philologists and certain early Indian commentators derive it from the adjective parama (supreme/highest) combined with the suffix -itā, meaning "perfection" or "excellence". However, Tibetan translators and indigenous Mahāyāna hermeneutics universally interpreted the morphology as pāram (to the other shore) + ita (gone). This soteriological metaphor deeply informed the Tibetan calque pha rol tu phyin pa (gone to the other shore). The Chinese tradition, facing the same ambiguity, opted for the phonetic loan 波羅蜜多 (*pa-la-mit-ta), though Chinese commentaries frequently elucidate the term using the "other shore" metaphor. In the context of the Heart Sutra, this concept is invariably paired with prajñā to denote the transcendent nature of Avalokiteśvara's practice. It is not merely wisdom, but wisdom that has crossed beyond the boundaries of conceptual elaboration and dualistic grasping.

The sutra thus establishes that the realization of emptiness is not a static intellectual milestone, but an active, continuous behavioral modality that pervades the bodhisattva's compassionate engagement with the world.

**Notes:** This adjective modifies prajñāpāramitā directly at the outset of the sutra. The descriptor gambhīra (profound) is not a mere poetic embellishment; it is a technical marker utilized in Mahāyāna literature to differentiate the ultimate realization of emptiness from lesser, conventional forms of wisdom. It flags the cognitive shift from the conventional truth (saṃvṛtisatya) to the ultimate truth (paramārthasatya). When phenomena are understood merely in terms of their dependent origination on a mechanical level, the insight is considered "vast" but not necessarily "profound." It becomes gambhīra when the practitioner recognizes the radical lack of inherent existence (svabhāva) in all constituent elements of reality. Thus, Avalokiteśvara's practice is profound precisely because it plumbs the absolute depths of ontology, bypassing superficial Abhidharmic categorization to touch the unconditioned reality of śūnyatā.

**Contested:** yes (morphological reconstruction)
**Notes:** The translation of this name is one of the most famous philological debates in Buddhist lexicography. Early Chinese translators, notably Kumārajīva, rendered the name as Guānyīn (觀音, "Perceiver of Sounds") or Guānshìyīn (觀世音, "Perceiver of the World's Sounds"). This assumes an underlying Sanskrit compound of Avalokita (looking/perceiving) + svara (sound). Modern discoveries of early Gilgit manuscripts confirm that Avalokitasvara was indeed the original Indian form. However, by the time Xuanzang translated the Heart Sutra (T251), the Sanskrit tradition had altered the name to Avalokiteśvara, replacing svara (sound) with īśvara (Lord/Master), reflecting growing Hindu/Shaivite influence. Consequently, Xuanzang corrected the translation to Guānzìzài (觀自在, "Lord of Gazing" or "Unrestricted Observer"). The Tibetan calque spyan ras gzigs dbang phyug strictly follows this later īśvara (dbang phyug) morphology. It is deeply fitting that the Bodhisattva of Compassion is the one to realize the ultimate emptiness of the aggregates, linking the two wings of Mahāyāna: profound wisdom and infinite compassion.

**Notes:** The term encapsulates the ideal practitioner of the Mahāyāna path. While Chinese translators transliterated the term entirely (pútísàduǒ, commonly abbreviated to púsà), Tibetans meticulously calqued it. Notably, the Tibetan lexicographers interpreted the Sanskrit component sattva not merely in its primary sense of "being" or "essence," but rather via a secondary Sanskrit nuance of "courage," "strength," or "hero" (śūra). Thus, they rendered it as sems dpa' (courageous mind/hero), making the full term byang chub sems dpa' (awakening-mind-hero). In the Heart Sutra, the term is frequently compounded as bodhisattva mahāsattva (great being), emphasizing the vast, heroic scope of Avalokiteśvara's vow to liberate all beings. The sutra presents the bodhisattva not as a mythical deity, but as an epistemic role model demonstrating how one must perceive reality to achieve liberation.

**Notes:** The five skandhas are form (rūpa), feeling (vedanā), perception (saṃjñā), mental formations (saṃskāra), and consciousness (vijñāna). According to traditional Buddhist psychological models, these "heaps" or "piles" are the fundamental components of subjective experience. Older Chinese translations (like Kumārajīva's) occasionally used 陰 (yīn, "shade" or "covering"), implying that these aggregates obscure the truth. However, Xuanzang codified 蘊 (yùn, "heap" or "accumulation") as the definitive scholastic rendering, matching the Sanskrit literal meaning of a pile (often metaphorically likened by the Buddha to a pile of rice grains). The sutra's central philosophical bombshell is Avalokiteśvara's realization that all five skandhas are entirely empty of inherent existence (svabhāva). They are not solid building blocks of a person, but transient, dependently arisen processes.

**Notes:** Svabhāva is the primary ontological target of Madhyamaka negation. In early Abhidharma thought, while the "self" (ātman) was denied, the fundamental atomic constituents of reality (dharmas) were posited to have their own unique, defining essence or characteristic (svabhāva). The Prajñāpāramitā literature, and Nāgārjuna's subsequent systematization, launched a devastating critique against this concept. In the Heart Sutra, emptiness (śūnyatā) is not a void of absolute non-existence or a physical vacuum; it is specifically defined as the absence of svabhāva (svabhāva-śūnyān). The realization is that phenomena arise dependently (pratītyasamutpāda), and because they depend on causes and conditions, they absolutely lack an isolated, intrinsic, independent essence.

**Notes:** Śūnyatā is arguably the most contested and frequently misunderstood concept in Mahāyāna Buddhism. Misunderstandings of kōng (空) in early Chinese Buddhism often conflated it with the indigenous Daoist concept of wu (無, absolute non-being or void), leading to early nihilistic misinterpretations. It required later scholastic rigor by rigorous translators like Xuanzang to specify that śūnyatā implies dependent origination rather than nihilism. The Tibetan calque stong pa nyid is particularly precise, appending the abstract nominalizer nyid (representing the Sanskrit -tā, "-ness") to stong pa (empty) to ensure it is understood as an abstract philosophical state ("emptiness") rather than a physical space. The Heart Sutra famously declares that "form is emptiness, emptiness is form," effectively asserting that the lack of inherent existence is the very property that allows form to manifest.

**Notes:** In East Asian transliterations, Śāri (his mother's name) is transliterated phonetically, while putra (son) is calqued directly as 子 (zǐ, son), creating a hybrid name. Śāriputra's presence as the primary interlocutor and addressee in the Heart Sutra is a deliberate, highly ironic Mahāyāna literary device. In mainstream Buddhist literature, Śāriputra is the undisputed paragon of wisdom and the chief architect of the Abhidharma—the system that categorized reality into discrete, inherently existent dharmas. By having Avalokiteśvara instruct Śāriputra on the emptiness of those very categories, the sutra performs a profound subversion. It establishes the supremacy of Mahāyāna transcendent prajñā over Mainstream Buddhist scholastic analysis, effectively telling the wisest of the arhats that his foundational framework is ultimately illusory.

**Contested:** yes (origin, language, and primacy of recensions)
**Notes:** The Heart Sutra circulates in two primary architectural recensions: the "Short" text and the "Long" text. The Short text, standardized most famously in Chinese by Xuanzang (T251) and earlier by Kumārajīva (T250), lacks the traditional sutra framing. It dives immediately into Avalokiteśvara's realization. The Long text (found in the Tibetan Kangyur and Edward Conze's Sanskrit editions) includes the standard evam maya shrutam ("Thus have I heard") opening, establishes a setting at Vulture Peak with the Buddha in samādhi, and concludes with the Buddha arising to endorse Avalokiteśvara's teaching. Contemporary philologists, most notably Jan Nattier, robustly debate the text's genesis. The Nattier hypothesis argues that the "Sanskrit" short text was not an original Indian composition, but rather an apocryphal digest compiled in China from Kumārajīva's translation of the Large Prajñāpāramitā, which was subsequently back-translated into Sanskrit. This accounts for the Chinese idiomatic quirks present in the Sanskrit (like na jñānaṃ na prāptiḥ mapping perfectly to Kumārajīva's 無智亦無得). Regardless of its origin, Xuanzang's rhythmic, conceptually dense translation became the undisputed standard across East Asia, functioning simultaneously as high philosophy, protective talisman, and daily liturgy.
